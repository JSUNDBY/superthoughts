The approach

Your mind is more powerful than you think. Practice proves it.

Superthoughts creates guided audio programs rooted in mindfulness, awareness, and the science of habit change. Each program is a series of recordings you listen to at your own pace.

No courses to sit through. No homework. No rigid schedules. Just close your eyes, follow the guidance, and notice what shifts.

Practice, not theory

Every program is something you do, not something you study. Guided recordings you listen to and practice with in real moments of your day.

Built to last

Your pace, your terms

Backed by research

This isn't just a feeling. The science is clear.

The Nutrition Source

Mindful eating leads to greater psychological wellbeing, increased pleasure when eating, and body satisfaction.

+

Research from Harvard's Nutrition Source shows that mindful eating addresses shame and guilt by promoting a non-judgmental attitude toward food. It builds the skill of distinguishing emotional hunger from physical hunger, leading to more intentional food choices and a healthier relationship with eating.

PubMed, 68 studies reviewed

Mindfulness-based approaches are most effective at addressing binge eating, emotional eating, and eating in response to external cues.

+

A structured review of 68 published studies found that mindfulness-based interventions consistently outperform traditional approaches for reducing binge eating and emotional eating behaviors. The evidence suggests that awareness-based practices create lasting changes in how people relate to food and hunger signals.

Springer

Mindful eating has been associated with reduced anxiety, depression, and increased confidence.

+

Published research in Springer journals demonstrates that cultivating awareness around eating contributes to broader mental health outcomes. Participants in mindful eating programs showed measurable improvements in overall psychological wellbeing, not just their relationship with food.

Nature

Greater amounts of mindfulness practice lead to increased behavioral flexibility, breaking compulsive patterns.

+

Research published in Nature found that consistent mindfulness practice increases the brain's capacity for behavioral flexibility. This means a greater ability to pause, choose, and respond rather than react. For eating, this translates to breaking automatic patterns and making choices from clarity rather than compulsion.
